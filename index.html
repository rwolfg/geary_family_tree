The Mayflower connection

Three passengers · One financier · The ship that founded America — and your direct descent from it

"They knew they were pilgrims."

On September 16, 1620, the Mayflower departed Plymouth, England carrying 102 passengers across the North Atlantic. Among them were Francis Cooke and his wife Hester Mahieu — a married couple who both made the crossing — and separately Richard Warren, a merchant from Hertfordshire. All three survived. All three had children who had children who, through eleven generations, produced you.

The man who made the voyage financially possible — John Beauchamp, a London merchant and one of the principal investors in the Plymouth Company — was also your ancestor through his daughter Sarah Beauchamp, who emigrated to Rhode Island and married into your Spencer line. You carry the DNA of the passengers and the man who paid for the ship.

Your oldest documented ancestor, Thomas F. Spencer, was born in Eton, England in 1433 — nearly 600 years ago, when Henry VI was king. His descendants in six generations produced Gerard Spencer, whose son Thomas Spencer sailed to Rhode Island, whose daughter Sarah married into the Beauchamp line — connecting medieval Eton to Plymouth Rock in a single family tree.

Rhode Island founders in your tree

John Greene (1594–1659) was one of the co-founders of Warwick, Rhode Island alongside Roger Williams — the colony established on principles of religious tolerance that became one of America's foundational experiments in freedom of conscience. Greene emigrated from Gillingham, Dorset, England and helped purchase land from the Narragansett people to found Warwick in 1642.

His son Major John Greene (1620–1708) became a prominent colonial figure in Warwick — military officer, landowner, and community leader. His daughter Audrey Greene married William Spencer, producing the Spencer line that connects directly to your Nichols/Lydia Spencer branch.

This means you descend from a co-founder of Rhode Island — one of the original thirteen colonies and the first American experiment in separation of church and state.

Thomas F. Spencer: your oldest ancestor

Thomas F. Spencer, born 1433 in Eton, England, is your earliest documented ancestor — nearly 600 years ago. He was born during the reign of King Henry VI, four decades before Columbus sailed to America, and sixty years before Henry VIII was born.

His descendants over six generations — Robert Spencer → John Spencer (Edworth) → Michael Spencer → John Spencer (Bedfordshire) → Gerard Spencer (Stratford) → Thomas Spencer → all moved progressively toward London and then across the Atlantic to Rhode Island by the 1640s.

From medieval Eton to Plymouth Rock to Galveston, Texas: one family, 552 years.

The Huguenot thread: Hester Mahieu

Hester Mahieu was a French Huguenot — a Protestant refugee who fled Catholic persecution in France, settled in the Separatist community of Leiden, Netherlands, and then sailed on the Mayflower to Plymouth. The Mahieu (Mahieux, Maheu) family was French Walloon, from the southern Netherlands/Belgium region, making Hester the most Continental of your Mayflower ancestors.

This adds a fourth non-English strand to your colonial ancestry: alongside the English Jester and Schenck Dutch lines, Hester brings Walloon-French Huguenot heritage. At 90 years old, she was one of the longest-lived Mayflower passengers and outlived virtually every other survivor of the 1620 crossing.

The Melungeon connection

The Accomack County connection

Richard Jester settled Accomack County, Virginia around 1660. This county on Virginia's Eastern Shore was home to some of the first free African-descended families in all of colonial America — including the Anthony Johnson family, free Black landowners, from the 1640s. Scots-Irish settlers, free Africans, and Madeiran Portuguese sailors all coexisted here before the 1680s slave codes hardened racial categories.

When racial laws tightened after 1680, mixed-race families moved to the Appalachian frontier. The Jester line moved north to Delaware, then west through Ohio and Illinois — carrying their genetic heritage forward through each generation.
