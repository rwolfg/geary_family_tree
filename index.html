The Mayflower connection

Three passengers · One financier · The Rhode Island founders · And the oldest ancestor: Thomas Spencer, born 1433

"They knew they were pilgrims."

On September 16, 1620, the Mayflower departed Plymouth, England. Among the 102 passengers were Francis Cooke and his wife Hester Mahieu — a married couple who both made the crossing — and separately Richard Warren, a merchant from Hertfordshire. All three survived. The man who funded the voyage — John Beauchamp, London merchant — was also your ancestor through his daughter Sarah Beauchamp. Your Rhode Island colonial ancestors John Greene (co-founder of Warwick alongside Roger Williams, 1642) and Major John Greene connect the founding of religious tolerance in America to your direct family line. And behind it all: Thomas F. Spencer, born Eton 1433 — nearly 600 years ago.

Rhode Island founders

John Greene (1594–1659) emigrated from Gillingham, Dorset, England and co-founded Warwick, Rhode Island in 1642 alongside Roger Williams — one of America's first experiments in religious freedom. His son Major John Greene (1620–1708) became prominent in Warwick. Major Greene's daughter Audrey married William Spencer (1672) — connecting the Greene founder line to the Spencer-Beauchamp-Mayflower chain that reaches you through Lydia Spencer Nichols.

Thomas F. Spencer: born 1433

Your oldest documented ancestor. Born in Eton, England in 1433 — under Henry VI, before Columbus sailed, before the printing press reached England. The Melungeon connection

A hidden tri-racial heritage from colonial Appalachian Virginia — and why your DNA points directly to it

"Neither white, nor Black, nor Indian — and all three."

The Melungeons were a tri-racial isolate community whose origins trace to the earliest decades of English colonial America — the same Virginia where your Jester ancestors settled in 1660. Modern DNA studies confirm: primarily Scots-Irish and English, with consistent Sub-Saharan African ancestry (2–5%) and a recurring Iberian/Portuguese/Madeiran signal. Your profile matches precisely.

Your genetic profile — 21% Central Scotland/N. Ireland + 1% Western Bantu + 1% Madeira — hits all three markers. Combined with the Jester line's documented settlement in Accomack County, Virginia (1660s) — the epicenter of early American racial mixing — this is a coherent genetic and historical case.
